Muscardine Disease Detected

Confidence: 92%

Muscardine is a fungal disease that can be highly contagious, causing silkworms to become stiff and powdery. It leads to significant crop loss and is often identified by the white, chalky appearance of the infected larvae. Immediate sanitation and isolation are required.

Prevention & Control

Immediate Control:

  • Isolate infected worms and cocoons immediately.
  • Disinfect the rearing room and all equipment with recommended fungicides.
  • Maintain proper ventilation and low humidity.

Long-term Prevention:

  • Practice strict hygiene, including pre- and post-crop disinfection.
  • Use quality, disease-free silkworm eggs from a certified source.
  • Ensure proper spacing and avoid overcrowding in the rearing beds.
  • Monitor temperature and humidity closely to prevent fungal growth.

For severe or persistent crop issues, consult with a sericulture expert or a government agricultural office.

Pebrine

A debilitating microsporidian disease that can be passed down from mother moth to egg.

Grasserie

A viral disease causing silkworms to appear sluggish and their skin to be shiny and fragile.

Flacherie

A bacterial disease causing loose body, vomiting, and a swollen appearance in silkworms.

Uzi Fly

A parasitic fly that lays eggs on silkworm bodies, a major pest in tropical regions.

Dandruff

A fungal infection in the rearing bed, causing silkworm body shrinkage and growth issues.
